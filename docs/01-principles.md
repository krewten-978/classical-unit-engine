# CUE Principles

## 1. The teacher remains the judge

CUE assists with research, organization, conversion, and production. It does not replace the teacher’s judgment. The teacher decides what is worth teaching, what is too hard, what is too easy, what is beautiful, what is central, and what students need next.

## 2. Texts before activities

A CUE unit begins with a real question and real texts. Activities exist to help students see, summarize, compare, draw, and argue from those texts.

## 3. Argument before coverage

A good unit does not merely “cover” authors. It stages a conflict of claims. Students should be able to answer:

- What does this author claim?
- Why does he or she think that?
- What does the author assume about human nature, God, society, freedom, knowledge, honor, power, or virtue?
- Who in the unit disagrees?
- Which view can I defend?

## 4. Difficulty should be judged, not guessed

Before adapting a source, CUE must judge the actual source difficulty:

- Is the translation already close enough for students with support?
- Is the syntax archaic or tangled enough to block comprehension?
- Are the ideas difficult but the prose manageable?
- Are the words simple but the argument subtle?

This judgment determines whether to use a faithful excerpt, light support, full modernization, or a parallel original/paraphrase format.

## 5. Differentiate access, not substance

10th grade students may need shorter excerpts, more vocabulary support, clearer section headings, and more guided writing tasks. They should not receive babyish versions of serious ideas.

12th grade students may read longer excerpts and carry more of the comparison and counterargument work. They still need clear framing and purposeful support.

## 6. Every task should produce something

CUE avoids worksheet-shaped tasks. Student work should produce one of the following:

- a claim
- a summary
- a window note
- a paragraph
- an argument map
- a dialogue
- a comparison
- a seminar preparation note
- a paper thesis
- a full argument paper

## 7. Sources must be auditable

Every source card should preserve enough information to check the passage later:

- author
- work
- section/chapter/book/line if possible
- translation/editor if relevant
- URL or publication information
- why the passage belongs in the unit

## 8. Fewer strong sources beat many weak sources

A CUE unit should not become a dump of famous names. A small set of strong, opposed, teachable readings is better than a large packet that students cannot intellectually inhabit.

## 9. Modernization preserves argument

Modernized prose is not a summary. It should preserve:

- the order of thought
- the key claim
- the reasons
- the tension
- the imagery where important
- the author’s terms when they matter

## 10. The final product should make the unit cohere

The final student product should force students to use the unit as a whole. Usually this means an argument paper requiring textual evidence and a fair response to an opposing view.
