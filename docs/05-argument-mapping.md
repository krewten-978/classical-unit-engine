# Argument Mapping Standard

Argument maps make the intellectual structure of a unit visible. They prepare students for comparison, seminar, and paper writing.

## Map each major reading

For each thinker or text, identify:

- central claim
- supporting reasons
- assumptions
- key passage
- what this view explains well
- what this view struggles to explain
- strongest opponent
- student thesis this could support

## Good central claims

A good central claim is debatable and precise.

Weak:

- “Hobbes talks about freedom.”

Stronger:

- “Hobbes argues that freedom means the absence of external impediments, not freedom from causes or desires.”

## Assumptions to look for

Depending on the unit, look for assumptions about:

- human nature
- reason
- appetite or desire
- virtue
- God or providence
- political authority
- natural law
- fate
- freedom
- responsibility
- knowledge
- death
- honor
- justice

## Comparison maps

A comparison map should show conflict, not just list summaries.

Use categories such as:

- definition of the key term
- view of human nature
- source of authority
- account of responsibility
- strongest evidence
- likely objection
- best opponent

## Student-facing use

Argument maps can become student tasks, but should still require thinking. Good tasks include:

- write the central claim in one sentence
- choose the best evidence sentence
- identify the strongest objection
- defend this thinker against another
- write a dialogue between two thinkers
- turn the map into a thesis paragraph
