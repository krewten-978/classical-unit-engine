## Core Unit Question

**Can human beings be morally responsible if our actions are shaped by fate, nature, causes, or desire?**

## Supporting Teacher Questions

- What makes an action truly "ours"?
- When should fear, ignorance, passion, habit, or outside pressure reduce blame?
- Can an action be caused and still be voluntary?
- Does divine providence or scientific causation leave room for guilt, praise, blame, and choice?
- Are characters such as Oedipus and Macbeth less responsible, more responsible, or differently responsible because prophecy and circumstance surround their actions?

## Unit Thesis

This unit should not ask students simply to choose between "free will" and "fate" as slogans. It should teach them to distinguish kinds of cause, kinds of ignorance, kinds of desire, and kinds of responsibility. Across the unit, students should see that the hardest question is not whether actions have causes, but whether some caused actions can still belong to the person who performs them.

## Why This Unit Follows "Do We Love Shadows?"

The first unit asks whether human beings want truth or prefer comforting shadows. This second unit asks a natural next question: if truth can be known, are we free enough to live by it?

Students now move from the problem of knowing to the problem of choosing. Plato, Homer, Bacon, Augustine, Kant, and Nietzsche gave students language for illusion, desire, courage, and truth. Unit 2 extends that language into responsibility: when fear, desire, ignorance, habit, prophecy, nature, or necessity shape a person's action, can that person still be praised or blamed?

## Unit Arc

### Beginning: Responsibility Needs Distinctions

Aristotle and Cicero open the unit by slowing students down. They prevent shallow answers by giving students distinctions: voluntary and involuntary action, compulsion and mixed action, ignorance of facts and ignorance of the good, fate and causation, prediction and necessity, assent and outside influence.

The opening movement should train students not to ask only, "Was there a cause?" but rather, "What kind of cause was it, and did it remove responsibility?"

### Middle: Freedom, Evil, and the Ordered Will

Augustine and Aquinas move the question into Christian and medieval moral theology. The will is not treated as random self-assertion. It is ordered or disordered toward goods. A person is responsible not only for isolated acts, but for the loves, habits, laws, and ends that shape action.

The middle movement should help students ask whether freedom means doing whatever one wants, or whether true freedom requires reason, rightly ordered love, and the pursuit of the good.

### End: Compatibility and Determinist Pressure

Hobbes and Hume give early modern compatibilist accounts: liberty may not require an uncaused will. A person may be free when acting according to his own will, even if that will has causes. Laplace then presses the hardest scientific version of determinism: if every force and position were known, perhaps all past and future could be calculated.

The ending movement should put pressure on the whole ladder. Students should ask whether responsibility survives if human action fits inside a complete causal order.

## Reading Ladder

### Reading 1: Aristotle — Voluntary Action

- **Text:** *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book III, chapters 1--5.
- **Function:** Foundation reading for responsibility, praise, blame, pardon, and habit.
- **Problem:** Not every caused, pressured, ignorant, or passionate action should be judged the same way.
- **Student question:** If fear, ignorance, desire, and habit shape our actions, are those actions still ours?
- **Student product:** Window note distinguishing voluntary, involuntary, and mixed action; 3--5 sentence summary of praise, blame, pardon, and pity.
- **Build status:** Complete as `student-packets/01_aristotle_voluntary_action_handout.pdf`.

### Reading 2: Cicero — Fate and Responsibility

- **Text:** *On Fate* selected sections.
- **Function:** Roman bridge from Aristotle's ethics to later debates about fate, causation, providence, and determinism.
- **Problem:** If all things are connected by causes, students may assume responsibility disappears; Cicero complicates that assumption.
- **Student question:** Does the existence of causes mean that our choices are not really ours?
- **Student product:** Cause-chain diagram, cylinder analogy explanation, and claim about whether prediction or fate removes responsibility.
- **Build status:** Complete as `student-packets/02_cicero_on_fate_handout.pdf`.

### Reading 3: Augustine — Free Choice and Evil

- **Text:** *On Free Choice of the Will*, Book I selections.
- **Function:** Christian account of moral evil, disordered desire, temporal law, eternal law, reason, and will.
- **Problem:** If God is good, why is there moral evil? Augustine's answer depends on free choice and the will's turning toward lower goods.
- **Student question:** If desire pulls us toward lower goods, are we still responsible for choosing them?
- **Student product:** Window note showing the will turning toward eternal goods or changeable goods; comparison claim using Aristotle or Cicero.
- **Build status:** Complete as `student-packets/03_augustine_free_choice_handout.pdf`.

### Reading 4: Thomas Aquinas — Reason, Will, Habit, Law, and Final Ends

- **Text:** *Summa Theologiae* I--II selected articles on voluntary action, human acts, law, habit, and final ends.
- **Function:** Medieval synthesis. Aquinas should connect Aristotle's voluntary action, Augustine's ordered love, and the larger question of whether freedom is completed by reason and the good.
- **Problem:** Students may think freedom means absence of limits. Aquinas gives a stronger account: human action aims at ends, and law/habit can form the will toward or away from its proper good.
- **Student question:** Is freedom the ability to choose anything, or the ability to choose the good rationally?
- **Student product:** Reason-will-end map; claim comparing Aquinas with Augustine or Aristotle.
- **Build status:** Planned next student packet.

### Reading 5: Thomas Hobbes — Liberty and Necessity

- **Text:** *Leviathan* selections on liberty, necessity, deliberation, and voluntary action.
- **Function:** Early modern compatibilist challenge.
- **Problem:** Hobbes separates freedom from uncaused willing. A person may be free when no external impediment blocks action, even if desire and will have causes.
- **Student question:** Can an action be necessary and still free?
- **Student product:** Definition comparison: Aristotle's voluntary action, Augustine's free choice, Hobbes's liberty.
- **Build status:** Planned.

### Reading 6: David Hume — Liberty and Necessity

- **Text:** *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, Section VIII.
- **Function:** Post-1700 compatibilist refinement.
- **Problem:** Hume argues that necessity and liberty have been misunderstood. Regular causes in human behavior may be necessary for moral judgment, not destructive of it.
- **Student question:** Does responsibility require actions to be uncaused, or does it require reliable connections between character, motive, action, and consequence?
- **Student product:** Compatibilist argument map; objection and reply paragraph.
- **Build status:** Planned.

### Reading 7: Laplace — Scientific Determinism

- **Text:** *A Philosophical Essay on Probabilities*, Chapter 1.
- **Function:** Final determinist pressure point.
- **Problem:** Laplace imagines an intellect that, knowing every force and position in nature, could calculate the whole past and future. Students must ask what room remains for choice, guilt, praise, or blame.
- **Student question:** If the whole future could in principle be calculated, would moral responsibility survive?
- **Student product:** Final determinism challenge paragraph and seminar preparation.
- **Build status:** Planned.

## Literary Test Cases

### Sophocles, *Oedipus Rex*

Use Oedipus as the tragic pressure test for prophecy, ignorance, and responsibility. The play should not be treated as a simple example of "no free will." It is better as a case where prophecy, ignorance, pride, investigation, and tragic discovery all meet.

Possible teacher questions:

- If Oedipus does not know whom he kills or marries, what kind of ignorance is involved?
- Would Aristotle call Oedipus's acts involuntary, mixed, or blameworthy in different ways?
- Does prophecy cause Oedipus's actions, or reveal them?
- Is Oedipus guilty, polluted, tragic, pitiable, responsible, or some combination?

### Shakespeare, *Macbeth*

Use Macbeth as the more direct moral-responsibility test. Macbeth hears prophecy, but he also imagines murder, chooses concealment, yields to ambition, and acts repeatedly after the first crime.

Possible teacher questions:

- Do the witches cause Macbeth's actions, or awaken his desires?
- Does ambition reduce Macbeth's responsibility or reveal his character?
- How would Aristotle judge Macbeth's first murder compared with his later murders?
- How would Augustine describe Macbeth's disordered desire?
- Would Hobbes or Hume say Macbeth is free if he acts according to his will, even if his will has causes?

## Suggested Student Work Sequence

### After Reading 1: Aristotle

Window note: voluntary, involuntary, and mixed action. Short claim: When does pressure reduce blame, and when does it not?

### After Reading 2: Cicero

Cause-chain diagram: fate, nature, chance, assent, and responsibility. Short claim: Does a chain of causes remove responsibility?

### After Reading 3: Augustine

Two-directions-of-the-will drawing: eternal goods and changeable goods. Short claim: Why does Augustine think God is not the cause of moral evil?

### After Reading 4: Aquinas

Reason-will-end map: what the person seeks, what reason judges, what habit makes easier, and what law orders. Short claim: Is law an enemy of freedom or a teacher of freedom?

### After Reading 5: Hobbes

Definition comparison: liberty as absence of external impediment vs. freedom as moral self-command. Short claim: Can necessity and liberty coexist?

### After Reading 6: Hume

Compatibilist argument map: necessity, character, motive, action, responsibility. Short objection-and-reply paragraph.

### After Reading 7: Laplace

Determinism challenge: if an intellect could calculate every event, what would happen to praise and blame? Seminar claim using at least two earlier authors.

## Assessment Direction

The current scope sequence proposes a Socratic seminar synthesis plus thesis paragraph focused on Macbeth:

**Is Macbeth more responsible, less responsible, or differently responsible if prophecy, ambition, imagination, character, and circumstance all shape his actions?**

This is a good 10th-grade culminating product because it requires students to apply the philosophical ladder to a literary case rather than merely report each philosopher's view.

Possible student positions include:

1. **Aristotle position:** Macbeth is responsible because his actions proceed from his own desire, deliberation, and formed character, even under pressure.
2. **Cicero position:** Prophecy and causes do not erase responsibility; the decisive question is how Macbeth assents and acts within the causal chain.
3. **Augustine position:** Macbeth's evil comes from disordered love: ambition, honor, power, and security become higher to him than justice and goodness.
4. **Aquinas position:** Macbeth is unfree in the deeper sense because his will is disordered from its proper end, even while he voluntarily chooses particular acts.
5. **Hobbes/Hume position:** Macbeth may be free if he acts according to his will without external restraint, even if his will has causes; responsibility can survive necessity.
6. **Laplace challenge:** If Macbeth's whole character and future were calculable from prior causes, students must decide whether moral judgment is still meaningful.

## Assessment Standards

Student arguments should be evaluated for:

- clear thesis answering the responsibility question
- accurate use of at least two unit readings
- application to a literary case, not only abstract summary
- fair explanation of a serious opposing view
- distinction between cause and excuse
- distinction between pressure, ignorance, desire, habit, and necessity
- explanation of textual evidence
- conclusion that returns to moral responsibility
