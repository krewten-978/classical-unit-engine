# Do We Love Shadows?
## Reading 4: Frederick Douglass and Literacy as Liberation

### Vocabulary Preview

- **Literacy:** The ability to read and write.
- **Enslaved:** Held as property and denied freedom.
- **Master:** A person who claims ownership and power over an enslaved person.
- **Liberation:** Being set free.
- **Ignorance:** Lack of knowledge or understanding.
- **Tyranny:** Cruel or unjust power over others.
- **Consciousness:** Awareness of oneself and one's condition.
- **Dehumanize:** To treat a person as less than fully human.
- **Forbidden:** Not allowed.
- **Abolition:** The movement to end slavery.

### Introduction

Frederick Douglass was born into slavery in Maryland around 1818 and escaped to freedom in 1838. He became one of the most powerful writers and speakers against slavery in American history. In his *Narrative*, Douglass explains how learning to read changed his understanding of himself, slavery, and freedom.

Douglass helps us see that shadows are not always accidental. Sometimes people are kept in darkness on purpose. If knowledge can awaken a person to freedom, then unjust power may try to prevent that knowledge.

As you read, ask this question: **Why would someone in power want others kept in darkness?**

## Reading: Learning to Read

When I was sent to live in Baltimore, my new mistress began to teach me the alphabet. She was kind at first and did not yet understand the full habits of slavery. After I learned some letters and began spelling small words, my master discovered what was happening.

He immediately forbade her to continue.

He said it was unlawful and unsafe to teach an enslaved person to read. He said that if I learned to read, I would become unfit to be a slave. He said learning would make me discontented and unhappy. He said it would do me no good, but only harm.

His words sank deep into my mind. They were meant to stop my education, but they revealed something important. I now understood the pathway from slavery to freedom.

If my master feared my learning, then learning must have power. If reading would make me unfit to be a slave, then reading could help make me fit to be a free man.

From that moment, I was determined to learn.

My mistress stopped teaching me, but I found other ways. I carried bread with me and traded it with poor white boys in the neighborhood for lessons. Many of them were hungry. I gave them bread, and they gave me knowledge. I learned from walls, fences, scraps of writing, shipyard marks, and any printed word I could find.

As I learned to read, I began to understand more clearly the evil of slavery. Books and speeches against slavery gave words to what I already felt. They showed me arguments about liberty and human rights. They helped me see that slavery was not natural, just, or permanent.

But learning also brought pain. Before I could read, I suffered without fully understanding the system that held me. After I learned, I saw my condition more clearly. I understood the injustice of slavery, but I did not yet know how to escape it.

At times, reading made me miserable. It opened my eyes, but I was still in chains. I envied the ignorance I once had, because knowledge made the cruelty of slavery more visible.

Yet I could not go back. Once I had seen more clearly, I could not unsee. The light wounded me, but it also awakened me.

I continued learning to write by copying letters at the shipyard and challenging boys to writing contests. Step by step, I gained the tools that slavery had tried to deny me.

My master's command had taught me the truth: slavery depends on darkness. It needs the enslaved person kept from reading, kept from writing, kept from knowing history, law, argument, and the meaning of liberty.

Education did not instantly free my body. But it began freeing my mind. It made slavery hateful to me. It taught me to think of myself not as property, but as a man made for freedom.

## Closing Thought

Douglass shows that education is not only personal improvement. It can be liberation. But he also shows why truth is dangerous to unjust power: people who see clearly may no longer consent to their chains.
