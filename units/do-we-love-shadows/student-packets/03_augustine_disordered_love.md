# Do We Love Shadows?
## Reading 3: Augustine and Disordered Love

### Vocabulary Preview

- **Confession:** An honest admission of truth before God and others.
- **Restless:** Unable to be at peace.
- **Desire:** A strong longing for something.
- **Disordered love:** Loving a lesser thing too much, or loving things in the wrong order.
- **Praise:** Approval or admiration from others.
- **Ambition:** The desire for honor, success, or power.
- **Conversion:** A turning of the soul toward God or truth.
- **Vanity:** Empty pride in appearances, reputation, or success.
- **Conscience:** The inward awareness of right and wrong.
- **Delight:** Deep pleasure or joy in something.

### Introduction

Augustine lived from A.D. 354 to 430. In *Confessions*, he looks back on his life and tells the truth about his desires, sins, ambitions, and search for God. Augustine is not only interested in wrong ideas. He is interested in wrong loves.

Plato says people may mistake shadows for reality. Bacon says the mind is distorted by idols. Augustine adds another possibility: sometimes we resist truth because we love something else more. We may love praise, pleasure, success, control, or our own image. In that case, truth is not merely hard to understand. It is hard to want.

As you read, ask this question: **What if we resist truth because we love the wrong things?**

## Reading: Loving Shadows

You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our heart is restless until it rests in you.

When I look back on my life, I see that I often searched for happiness in the wrong places. I wanted to be admired. I wanted to be praised. I wanted to seem successful in the eyes of others. I wanted the applause of the crowd more than the quiet truth of my own soul.

As a boy, I was often praised when I spoke well, even if my heart was not good. People cared that I learned the arts of speech, but they did not always care whether I loved wisdom. I learned to fear shame more than sin. I learned to desire victory more than truth.

This was one of my shadows: I wanted to appear excellent more than I wanted to become excellent.

Later, I chased after pleasures that promised joy but left me divided. I knew, at times, that I was not at peace. Yet I did not want to be healed too quickly. I prayed, in effect, "Give me purity and self-control, but not yet." I wanted truth, but I also wanted to keep the pleasures that truth would ask me to surrender.

This is a strange sickness of the soul: to know the better path and still cling to the worse one.

I was not held by iron chains. I was held by my own will. Habit had become strong because I had obeyed desire for so long. Desire became habit, and habit became necessity. I was bound, not because someone else chained me, but because I had learned to love my chains.

I also loved praise. Praise can be a dangerous shadow. It makes a person ask, "How do I appear?" instead of "What is true?" A person may begin by wanting to do good, but slowly begin to love being seen as good more than goodness itself.

The soul becomes bent when it loves lower things as if they were highest things. Food, friendship, beauty, success, learning, and honor can be good in their place. But when any created thing becomes the highest love, the soul becomes restless. It tries to make a small thing carry the weight of ultimate happiness.

That is why truth can feel painful. Truth does not merely correct our thoughts. It corrects our loves. It asks us to give up false happiness. It asks us to stop pretending that shadows can satisfy the heart.

When I finally began to turn toward God, I did not experience it as learning a new fact only. It was more like being healed, and healing can hurt. I had to admit that many things I loved had kept me from freedom.

The problem was not that truth was ugly. The problem was that I had become used to lesser beauties. The light was good, but my eyes were weak. Wisdom was good, but my loves were disordered.

The soul becomes free when it learns to love things in the right order.

## Closing Thought

Plato shows that truth may hurt the eyes. Bacon shows that the mind may distort what it sees. Augustine shows that the heart may resist truth because it has learned to love its shadows.
