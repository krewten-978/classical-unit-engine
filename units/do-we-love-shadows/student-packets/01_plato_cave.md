# Do We Love Shadows?
## Reading 1: Plato and the Cave

### Vocabulary Preview

- **Allegory:** A story whose details stand for deeper ideas.
- **Illusion:** Something false or misleading that seems real.
- **Reality:** What is actually true, even if people do not see it clearly.
- **Prisoner:** In this reading, a person trapped by false appearances.
- **Shadow:** A copy, image, or appearance mistaken for the real thing.
- **Ascent:** A climb upward; here, the difficult movement from ignorance toward truth.
- **Dazzled:** Blinded or overwhelmed by sudden light.
- **Compelled:** Forced or strongly pushed to do something.
- **Enlightened:** Given understanding or clearer vision.
- **The Good:** For Plato, the highest truth that helps us understand everything else.

### Introduction

Plato was a Greek philosopher who lived in Athens from about 428 to 348 B.C. In *Republic*, he asks what justice is, what kind of city is best, and what kind of education forms a good human being. One of the most famous parts of *Republic* is the Allegory of the Cave.

In this passage, Socrates asks us to imagine people who have lived their whole lives chained inside a cave. They can only see shadows on a wall, so they mistake those shadows for reality. The story is about education, but not education as memorizing information. For Plato, education is a painful turning of the whole person away from illusion and toward truth.

As you read, ask this question: **Why might someone prefer shadows to truth?**

## Reading: The Cave

Imagine human beings living in an underground cave. They have been there since childhood. Their legs and necks are chained so that they cannot move around or turn their heads. They can only look straight ahead at the wall of the cave.

Behind them, higher up and at a distance, there is a fire burning. Between the fire and the prisoners is a raised path. Along that path, other people carry objects: statues of people and animals, figures made of wood and stone, and many other things. The fire casts the shadows of these objects onto the wall in front of the prisoners.

Because the prisoners cannot turn around, they do not see the fire, the path, the carriers, or the objects. They only see the shadows. If the people carrying the objects speak, their voices echo through the cave. The prisoners think the voices come from the shadows themselves.

To the prisoners, the shadows are not shadows. The shadows are the world. If they had contests, they would honor the prisoner who best remembered which shadows usually came first, which came later, and which appeared together. That prisoner would seem wise inside the cave.

But what would happen if one prisoner were freed?

Suppose someone released him from his chains and forced him to stand up, turn his head, walk, and look toward the fire. At first, the movement would hurt. The light would sting his eyes. He would not immediately understand what he was seeing. If someone told him that the shadows he used to see were less real, and that the objects behind him were more real, he would be confused.

He might even think the shadows were clearer and truer than the objects now shown to him.

Then suppose someone dragged him up the steep path out of the cave and into the sunlight. He would suffer. He would be angry at the person pulling him. When he reached the upper world, the sunlight would be so bright that he could not see the things around him.

Little by little, his eyes would adjust. First, he might see shadows. Then he might see reflections in water. After that, he could look at actual things: trees, animals, people, and the world itself. Later, he could look at the stars and moon at night. Finally, he might be able to see the sun itself.

Then he would understand that the sun makes sight possible. It gives light. It causes the seasons. It nourishes living things. In some way, it is the source of all the things he and the other prisoners had only seen dimly.

Now imagine that this freed prisoner remembers the cave. He remembers the old contests about the shadows. He remembers the honors given to those who understood the shadow-world best. Would he still care about those honors? Would he envy the prisoners who were praised inside the cave?

Probably not. He would rather suffer than go back to living by shadows.

But now suppose he returns to the cave. Coming suddenly out of the sunlight, his eyes would be filled with darkness. If he had to compete again in judging the shadows, he would do badly at first because he could no longer see well in the dark.

The other prisoners would laugh at him. They would say that his journey upward had ruined his eyesight. They would say it was dangerous to leave the cave. And if someone tried to free them and lead them upward, they might resist. If they could, they might even attack the person trying to release them.

Socrates says this image shows something about education. The soul does not turn from darkness to light easily. Just as the whole body must turn around before the eye can see the light, the whole soul must be turned toward what is real and true.

Education is not simply putting sight into blind eyes. The power to learn is already present in the soul. But it is often facing the wrong direction. Education is the art of turning the soul around.

## Closing Thought

For Plato, education is not always comfortable. It may feel like being turned around, dazzled, corrected, or pulled away from what seems familiar. The question is whether the pain of truth is worth more than the comfort of shadows.
