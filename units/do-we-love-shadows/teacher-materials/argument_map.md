# Argument Map: Do We Love Shadows?

## Unit question

**Do human beings really want truth, or do we prefer comforting shadows?**

## Plato — The Cave

- **Central claim:** Human beings can mistake appearances for reality and may resist liberation because truth is painful and unfamiliar.
- **Because:**
  1. Prisoners know only the shadows they have always seen.
  2. Turning toward light hurts at first.
  3. Those still in the cave may mock or attack the one who returns.
- **Assumes:** Education requires a turning of the whole soul, not merely adding information.
- **What this view explains well:** Why people cling to familiar illusions.
- **Possible weakness:** It can make ignorance seem mainly accidental rather than desired, seductive, moral, or political.
- **Best unit companion:** Homer shows beautiful temptation; Bacon names distortions; Augustine deepens the motive of love.

## Homer — The Sirens

- **Central claim:** Human beings may be drawn toward destructive illusion because it appears beautiful and promises knowledge, delight, and distinction.
- **Because:**
  1. The Sirens' song is sweet, not obviously ugly.
  2. They promise Odysseus that he will go away wiser.
  3. Odysseus wants to hear even after he knows the danger.
  4. He survives only by arranging restraints before desire overwhelms judgment.
- **Assumes:** Desire can overpower knowledge; self-command sometimes requires outside help, discipline, or binding limits.
- **What this view explains well:** Why people may knowingly approach dangerous illusions.
- **Possible weakness:** The episode dramatizes temptation more than it explains the nature of truth itself.
- **Best unit companion:** Plato explains shadows as false reality; Augustine explains disordered love; Kant asks whether maturity requires self-rule.

## Bacon — The Four Idols

- **Central claim:** The mind is distorted by built-in human weakness, private bias, language, and inherited systems.
- **Because:**
  1. The human mind is like a crooked mirror.
  2. Personal caves shape perception.
  3. Words and systems can trap thought.
- **Assumes:** Better thinking requires diagnosing false images before pursuing truth.
- **What this view explains well:** Why intelligent people still reason badly.
- **Possible weakness:** It may understate the role of beauty, desire, sin, and courage.
- **Best unit companion:** Homer adds seductive beauty; Augustine adds disordered love; Nietzsche presses Bacon's language/idol problem further.

## Augustine — Disordered Love

- **Central claim:** People resist truth because their loves are disordered; they may prefer praise, pleasure, habit, or self-image to healing.
- **Because:**
  1. The heart is restless when it seeks ultimate happiness in lesser things.
  2. Habit can become a chain.
  3. Truth asks not only for new ideas but for conversion of desire.
- **Assumes:** Knowing the truth is not enough if the will loves something else more.
- **What this view explains well:** Why people knowingly avoid what is good.
- **Possible weakness:** It can focus so much on the soul that public systems of ignorance need separate attention.
- **Best unit companion:** Homer dramatizes desire overpowering judgment; Plato gives the image of conversion.

## Kant — Enlightenment and Courage

- **Central claim:** People remain in immaturity partly because it is comfortable to let others think for them; enlightenment requires courage to use one's own reason.
- **Because:**
  1. Laziness and cowardice make dependence easy.
  2. Guardians can make immaturity seem safe and normal.
  3. Freedom to reason publicly is necessary for enlightenment.
- **Assumes:** Human beings are responsible for the use or neglect of their rational powers.
- **What this view explains well:** Why people may prefer guided comfort to difficult independence.
- **Possible weakness:** It may understate the power of desire, beauty, habit, and social constraint.
- **Best unit companion:** Plato's prisoner needs turning; Homer's Odysseus needs restraint before he can safely hear; Nietzsche challenges whether inherited concepts themselves may be shadows.

## Nietzsche — Truth and Metaphor

- **Central claim:** What human beings call “truth” may often be a mobile army of metaphors, images, and conventions that people have forgotten are human-made.
- **Because:**
  1. Language turns unique experience into concepts.
  2. Concepts flatten difference and make life manageable.
  3. People forget the metaphorical origin of their truths.
- **Assumes:** Human beings are image-making creatures, not detached spectators of reality.
- **What this view explains well:** Why “truth” itself can become questionable.
- **Possible weakness:** Students may ask whether Nietzsche's own claim can escape the suspicion he applies to truth.
- **Best unit companion:** Plato gives a confident ascent toward truth; Nietzsche challenges that confidence.

## Major positions students can defend

### Position 1: People resist truth because truth hurts
- Strongest support: Plato, Augustine, Kant
- Possible objection: Homer would say people may also be attracted by beauty, not merely afraid of pain.

### Position 2: People love shadows because shadows can be beautiful
- Strongest support: Homer, Augustine, Plato
- Possible objection: Bacon would say the problem may begin in distortion before conscious desire.

### Position 3: People resist truth because their minds are distorted
- Strongest support: Bacon, Plato, Nietzsche
- Possible objection: Augustine would say the will and loves matter more than intellect alone.

### Position 4: People resist truth because they lack courage or self-command
- Strongest support: Kant, Homer, Plato
- Possible objection: Augustine would say the problem is deeper than courage; it is disordered love.

### Position 5: Education must form the whole person
- Strongest support: Plato, Homer, Augustine, Kant
- Possible objection: Nietzsche may question whether education can simply lead students from illusion into truth.


### Position 6: Truth may be human-made metaphor
- Strongest support: Nietzsche, Bacon, Plato
- Possible objection: Plato, Augustine, and Kant all depend on some real distinction between truth and illusion, not only useful metaphor.
