# Revised 10th Grade Classical English Scope & Sequence – Philosophy-Heavy

## 1. Course Identity

### Working Title
**The Examined Life: Classical Questions in Philosophy and Literature**

### One-Sentence Purpose
Students will enter the great conversation of classical education by studying a sequence of powerful philosophical questions, using major works of literature as dramatic tests of competing answers.

### Overarching Intellectual Thread
Across 2,500 years, students will ask whether human beings can know truth, act freely, become virtuous, rule justly, love rightly, suffer meaningfully, and live for something higher than appetite or success; each unit stages a dialogue among Greek, Roman, Christian/medieval, early modern, and post-1700 voices, while Homer, Virgil, Dante, Shakespeare, and other major literary works dramatize what is at stake.

## High-Level Year Outline

This year is designed as seven lean, philosophy-first units. Each unit follows the spirit of “Do We Love Shadows?”: one central Big Question, a focused reading ladder, a few literary illuminations, repeated seminar practice, and a culminating student product.

- **Semester 1: Knowing, Choosing, Becoming, Ruling**
  - Unit 1: Do We Love Shadows? truth, appearance, and education
  - Unit 2: Are We Free, or Are We Ruled by Fate? freedom and responsibility
  - Unit 3: What Makes a Soul Great? virtue, honor, and happiness
  - Unit 4: What Makes Rule Legitimate? justice, law, and political authority
- **Semester 2: Loving, Suffering, Living for an End**
  - Unit 5: What Do We Owe One Another? friendship, love, and moral duty
  - Unit 6: Can Suffering Teach Wisdom? death, evil, providence, and courage
  - Unit 7: What Is a Human Being For? nature, progress, technology, and the good life

## 2. Semester Structure + Weekly Map

## Semester 1

## Unit 1 — Do We Love Shadows?

### Central Big Question
**Do human beings really want truth, or do we prefer comforting shadows?**

### Pacing
- **Semester:** 1
- **Weeks:** 1–4
- **Length:** 4 weeks

### Philosophy Reading Ladder
1. **Plato, _Republic_ Book VII, Allegory of the Cave**
   - Establishes the founding image: education as painful liberation from illusion into reality.
2. **Lucretius, _On the Nature of Things_ Book II, opening image of looking from safety at storm and struggle**
   - Gives a Roman Epicurean counterpoint: philosophical vision frees the mind from fear, but may also detach the observer from ordinary human suffering.
3. **Augustine, _Confessions_ Book X, memory and inward truth**
   - Shifts the search for truth inward and upward: truth is not merely seen but loved, confessed, and ordered toward God.
4. **Francis Bacon, _Novum Organum_, “Idols of the Mind”**
   - Early modern diagnosis of false thinking: tribe, cave, marketplace, and theater distort the mind before inquiry even begins.
5. **Immanuel Kant, “What Is Enlightenment?”**
   - Post-1700 challenge: immaturity is often self-chosen; truth requires courage to use one’s own reason.
6. **Friedrich Nietzsche, “On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense”**
   - Modern provocation: perhaps what we call truth is a human-made system of metaphors we have forgotten are metaphors.

### Complementary Major Literature
- **Homer, _Odyssey_ Book XII, the Sirens** — desire, illusion, and disciplined listening.
- **Shakespeare, _The Tempest_ selected scenes** — art, illusion, education, and liberation.

### Proposed Final Student Product
**Comparative philosophical essay:** Which thinker gives the strongest account of why human beings cling to illusion?

### Sequence Rationale
This opening unit establishes the method of the course: one central question, a ladder of thinkers, and literature as a testing ground. It prepares students to ask whether knowledge is enough if the will is bound, which leads naturally into freedom and responsibility.

### Expected CUE Deliverables, High-Level Only
- Unit brief
- Source discovery packet / reading ladder
- Six short philosophy handouts or excerpt packets
- Literary illumination packet for Homer and Shakespeare
- Argument map comparing Plato, Bacon, Kant, and Nietzsche
- Final comparative essay prompt

## Unit 2 — Are We Free, or Are We Ruled by Fate?

### Central Big Question
**Can human beings be morally responsible if our actions are shaped by fate, nature, causes, or desire?**

### Pacing
- **Semester:** 1
- **Weeks:** 5–9
- **Length:** 5 weeks

### Philosophy Reading Ladder
1. **Aristotle, _Nicomachean Ethics_ Book III, voluntary and involuntary action**
   - Gives students a precise classical vocabulary for choice, compulsion, ignorance, praise, and blame.
2. **Cicero, _On Fate_ selected sections**
   - Brings a Roman voice into the debate over fate, causation, Stoicism, and moral responsibility.
3. **Augustine, _On Free Choice of the Will_ Book I selections**
   - Frames free will as necessary for moral responsibility while confronting evil and misdirected love.
4. **Thomas Aquinas, _Summa Theologiae_ I–II, questions on voluntary action and law, selected articles**
   - Gives the medieval synthesis: reason, will, habit, law, and final ends.
5. **Thomas Hobbes, _Leviathan_ selections on liberty and necessity**
   - Early modern counterclaim: freedom may mean acting according to one’s will even if the will has causes.
6. **David Hume, _An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding_ Section VIII, liberty and necessity**
   - Post-1700 compatibilist challenge: responsibility may survive causation if freedom is understood correctly.
7. **Pierre-Simon Laplace, _A Philosophical Essay on Probabilities_ Chapter 1, scientific determinism**
   - Adds the hard determinist pressure: if every force and position in nature were known, the whole past and future could be calculated.

### Complementary Major Literature
- **Sophocles, _Oedipus Rex_** — prophecy, ignorance, agency, and tragic responsibility.
- **Shakespeare, _Macbeth_ selected scenes** — prophecy, ambition, imagination, and chosen evil.

### Proposed Final Student Product
**Socratic seminar synthesis + thesis paragraph:** Is Macbeth more responsible, less responsible, or differently responsible if prophecy, ambition, imagination, character, and circumstance all shape his actions?

### Sequence Rationale
After asking whether we can know truth, students ask whether knowing truth would make us free. This unit deepens their ability to distinguish causes from excuses, then ends with Laplace's scientific determinist challenge: if nature is a chain of causes, what room remains for freedom, guilt, praise, or blame? The unit prepares them for the next question: if we are responsible, what should we become?

### Expected CUE Deliverables, High-Level Only
- Unit brief
- Reading ladder with 5–7 excerpt targets
- Handout plan for Aristotle, Cicero, Augustine, Aquinas, Hobbes, Hume, and Laplace
- Literary test case packet for Oedipus and Macbeth
- Argument map: libertarian, compatibilist, providential, tragic, and scientific determinist views
- Seminar synthesis task and thesis paragraph prompt

## Unit 3 — What Makes a Soul Great?

### Central Big Question
**Is greatness found in honor, virtue, self-command, happiness, or sacrifice?**

### Pacing
- **Semester:** 1
- **Weeks:** 10–13
- **Length:** 4 weeks

### Philosophy Reading Ladder
1. **Aristotle, _Nicomachean Ethics_ Book I and Book II selections**
   - Establishes happiness as activity of the soul in accordance with virtue and introduces habit as moral formation.
2. **Cicero, _On Duties_ Book I selections**
   - Romanizes virtue through duty, honor, public life, and moral obligation.
3. **Seneca, _Letters_ 41 or 76 selections**
   - Gives a Roman Stoic account of nobility, reason, and inner freedom independent of fortune.
4. **Augustine, _City of God_ Book XIX selections on peace and ordered love**
   - Challenges pagan virtue by asking whether the soul can be rightly ordered without the highest good.
5. **Michel de Montaigne, “Of Experience” or “That to Study Philosophy Is to Learn to Die” selections**
   - Early modern personalizes philosophy as self-knowledge, mortality, and disciplined judgment.
6. **Friedrich Nietzsche, _On the Genealogy of Morals_ Preface/First Essay selections**
   - Post-1700 provocation: are some moral ideals signs of strength, or masks for weakness and resentment?

### Complementary Major Literature
- **Homer, _Iliad_ Book IX or Book XXIV** — Achilles, honor, wrath, pity, and mortality.
- **Virgil, _Aeneid_ Book IV or Book VI** — Roman duty, cost, piety, and destiny.

### Proposed Final Student Product
**Comparative argument map:** Achilles, Aeneas, and one philosopher — which model of greatness is most worthy of imitation?

### Sequence Rationale
This unit builds from responsibility to formation. Students now ask not merely whether they choose, but what kind of soul their choices create. The unit prepares them to consider whether personal virtue is enough without just rule and shared law.

### Expected CUE Deliverables, High-Level Only
- Unit brief
- Philosophy ladder with virtue/honor/duty progression
- Focused excerpt handouts for Aristotle, Cicero, Seneca, Augustine, Montaigne, Nietzsche
- Literary comparison packet for Achilles and Aeneas
- Virtue and greatness argument map
- Comparative map product prompt

## Unit 4 — What Makes Rule Legitimate?

### Central Big Question
**When should human beings obey authority, and when must they resist it?**

### Pacing
- **Semester:** 1
- **Weeks:** 14–17
- **Length:** 4 weeks

### Philosophy Reading Ladder
1. **Plato, _Crito_ selections**
   - Opens with the classical problem of obedience to law, conscience, and the city.
2. **Cicero, _On the Republic_ or _On the Laws_ selections**
   - Strong Roman voice on natural law, commonwealth, and justice as the foundation of political order.
3. **Augustine, _City of God_ Book XIX or Book IV selections**
   - Asks whether kingdoms without justice are merely great robberies and relativizes earthly political hopes.
4. **Thomas Aquinas, _Summa Theologiae_ I–II on law, selected articles**
   - Medieval framework for eternal, natural, human, and divine law.
5. **Thomas Hobbes, _Leviathan_ chapters 13, 17, and 18 selections**
   - Early modern argument for sovereignty grounded in fear, self-preservation, and social contract.
6. **John Locke, _Second Treatise of Government_ chapters 2, 7, and 19 selections**
   - Post-1700 counterweight to Hobbes: natural rights, consent, limited government, and justified resistance.

### Complementary Major Literature
- **Sophocles, _Antigone_** — divine law, civic law, conscience, and burial.
- **Shakespeare, _Julius Caesar_ selected scenes** — republican virtue, ambition, assassination, and political rhetoric.

### Proposed Final Student Product
**Position paper:** Was Antigone right to disobey? Students must defend one political philosophy against another.

### Sequence Rationale
Having considered truth, freedom, and virtue, students move from the soul to the city. The unit closes Semester 1 by forcing students to connect private conscience to public order, preparing Semester 2’s turn toward love, obligation, suffering, and human ends.

### Expected CUE Deliverables, High-Level Only
- Unit brief
- Law and authority source ladder
- Handout plan for Plato, Cicero, Augustine, Aquinas, Hobbes, Locke
- Antigone/Julius Caesar literary testing packet
- Obedience/resistance argument map
- Position paper prompt

## Semester 2

## Unit 5 — What Do We Owe One Another?

### Central Big Question
**Is love a private feeling, a chosen friendship, a duty, or the highest form of justice?**

### Pacing
- **Semester:** 2
- **Weeks:** 18–22
- **Length:** 5 weeks

### Philosophy Reading Ladder
1. **Plato, _Symposium_ Diotima’s ladder of love selections**
   - Begins with eros as ascent from bodily desire toward beauty and truth.
2. **Aristotle, _Nicomachean Ethics_ Books VIII–IX selections on friendship**
   - Grounds love in virtue, mutual recognition, and shared life.
3. **Cicero, _On Friendship_ selections**
   - Roman account of friendship as moral loyalty, public virtue, and tested character.
4. **Augustine, _Confessions_ Book IV selections on grief and disordered love**
   - Shows love’s power and danger when finite goods are loved as ultimate.
5. **Thomas Aquinas, _Summa Theologiae_ II–II on charity, selected articles**
   - Medieval Christian account of love as charity ordered toward God and neighbor.
6. **Immanuel Kant, _Groundwork_ selections on duty and persons as ends**
   - Post-1700 reframes obligation: persons must never be treated merely as means.

### Complementary Major Literature
- **Dante, _Purgatorio_ or _Paradiso_ selected cantos** — love as the force that orders or disorders the soul.
- **Jane Austen, _Pride and Prejudice_ selected chapters** — judgment, affection, prudence, and moral education in love.

### Proposed Final Student Product
**Comparative seminar claim + written reflection:** Which account of love best protects both truth and loyalty?

### Sequence Rationale
After studying the city, students return to the human bonds that make life more than obedience. This unit asks whether love completes justice or conflicts with it, preparing students for suffering, grief, death, and the endurance of meaning.

### Expected CUE Deliverables, High-Level Only
- Unit brief
- Love/friendship/charity reading ladder
- Handout plan for Plato, Aristotle, Cicero, Augustine, Aquinas, Kant
- Dante/Austen literary illumination packet
- Love and obligation comparison map
- Seminar claim and reflection prompt

## Unit 6 — Can Suffering Teach Wisdom?

### Central Big Question
**Can suffering, death, and evil be made meaningful, or do they expose the limits of philosophy?**

### Pacing
- **Semester:** 2
- **Weeks:** 23–28
- **Length:** 6 weeks

### Philosophy Reading Ladder
1. **Plato, _Phaedo_ selections on philosophy and death**
   - Classical claim that philosophy prepares the soul to face death truthfully.
2. **Seneca, _Letters_ 24 or 70 selections on death and courage**
   - Roman Stoic training in courage, mortality, and freedom from fear.
3. **Boethius, _Consolation of Philosophy_ Book I and Book V selections**
   - Medieval bridge text on fortune, providence, and suffering under unjust power.
4. **Blaise Pascal, _Pensées_ selections on diversion, misery, and greatness**
   - Early modern Christian diagnosis of humanity as both wretched and great.
5. **David Hume, _Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion_ Part X selections**
   - Post-1700 philosophical challenge: suffering raises severe questions about providence and design.
6. **Friedrich Nietzsche, _Twilight of the Idols_ or _The Gay Science_ selections on suffering and strength**
   - Modern counterclaim: suffering may be transfigured by strength, creativity, and affirmation rather than explained away.

### Complementary Major Literature
- **The Book of Job** — innocent suffering, protest, silence, and divine answer.
- **Shakespeare, _King Lear_ selected scenes** — suffering, madness, blindness, compassion, and nothingness.

### Proposed Final Student Product
**Philosophical dialogue:** Students stage a conversation among three thinkers on whether suffering can teach wisdom.

### Sequence Rationale
The love unit raises the stakes of loss. Here students confront whether philosophy can console, explain, or transform suffering. This prepares the final unit, which asks what kind of life remains worth pursuing in the modern world.

### Expected CUE Deliverables, High-Level Only
- Unit brief
- Suffering/death/providence reading ladder
- Handout plan for Plato, Seneca, Boethius, Pascal, Hume, Nietzsche
- Job/King Lear literary testing packet
- Consolation and protest argument map
- Philosophical dialogue prompt

## Unit 7 — What Is a Human Being For?

### Central Big Question
**Are human beings made for virtue, freedom, progress, pleasure, power, holiness, or something else?**

### Pacing
- **Semester:** 2
- **Weeks:** 29–34
- **Length:** 6 weeks

### Philosophy Reading Ladder
1. **Aristotle, _Politics_ Book I and _Nicomachean Ethics_ Book X selections**
   - Classical account of human beings as rational, political, and ordered toward flourishing.
2. **Marcus Aurelius, _Meditations_ Books II, IV, and VI selections**
   - Roman Stoic account of living according to nature, reason, duty, and mortality.
3. **Thomas Aquinas, _Summa Contra Gentiles_ or _Summa Theologiae_ selections on final end and happiness**
   - Medieval account of the human end as ultimate happiness beyond finite goods.
4. **Francis Bacon, _New Atlantis_ selections**
   - Early modern reorientation toward knowledge, experiment, mastery, and the relief of man’s estate.
5. **Jean-Jacques Rousseau, _Discourse on the Sciences and Arts_ or _Discourse on Inequality_ selections**
   - Post-1700 critique of progress: civilization may corrupt rather than perfect human beings.
6. **John Stuart Mill, _On Liberty_ chapter 3 selections on individuality**
   - Post-1700 liberal account of human development through freedom, experiments in living, and individuality.

### Complementary Major Literature
- **Mary Shelley, _Frankenstein_ selected chapters** — knowledge, creation, ambition, responsibility, and human limits.
- **Shakespeare, _The Tempest_ selected return or review scenes** — revisits art, power, education, forgiveness, and freedom from Unit 1.

### Proposed Final Student Product
**Capstone argument paper:** What is the best answer the course has given to the question, “What is a human being for?” Students must use at least three philosophers and one literary work.

### Sequence Rationale
The final unit gathers the whole year. Truth, freedom, virtue, law, love, and suffering all converge in the question of human purpose. The year ends not with coverage, but with students entering the classical conversation through a defensible capstone thesis.

### Expected CUE Deliverables, High-Level Only
- Unit brief
- Human purpose/progress reading ladder
- Handout plan for Aristotle, Marcus Aurelius, Aquinas, Bacon, Rousseau, Mill
- Frankenstein/Tempest literary testing packet
- Full-year synthesis argument map
- Capstone paper prompt

# 3. Overall Student Production Rhythm Across the Year

## Weeks 1–4: Entering the Great Conversation
- Students learn to identify central claims, supporting images, and competing definitions.
- Main production: short comparative paragraphs, annotation marks, seminar claims, and a first comparative essay.

## Weeks 5–9: Distinguishing Claim, Cause, and Responsibility
- Students practice separating explanation from excuse and philosophical position from literary example.
- Main production: seminar synthesis, thesis paragraphs, short textual evidence chains.

## Weeks 10–13: Mapping Virtue and Character
- Students compare ideals of greatness using both philosophical claims and epic/dramatic examples.
- Main production: argument maps and comparative character-philosophy analysis.

## Weeks 14–17: Defending a Political Judgment
- Students learn to build a position paper: claim, philosopher A, philosopher B, literary test case, counterargument.
- Main production: obedience/resistance position paper.

## Weeks 18–22: Refining Moral Comparison
- Students handle abstract terms such as love, friendship, charity, duty, and personhood without reducing them to opinion.
- Main production: comparative seminar claim and reflective defense.

## Weeks 23–28: Staging Philosophical Conflict
- Students practice representing opponents fairly by writing philosophical dialogue.
- Main production: dialogue among thinkers, with textual grounding and explicit objections.

## Weeks 29–34: Capstone Synthesis
- Students return to the whole year and build an extended argument across multiple eras and genres.
- Main production: capstone paper using at least three philosophers and one literary work.

# 4. Balance Check

## Philosophy Coverage
- **Greek:** Plato and Aristotle anchor truth, freedom, virtue, politics, love, death, and human purpose.
- **Roman:** Lucretius, Cicero, Seneca, and Marcus Aurelius appear repeatedly as major voices, not decorations.
- **Medieval / Christian:** Augustine, Aquinas, Boethius, Dante, and Job keep theological and metaphysical questions central.
- **Early Modern:** Bacon, Hobbes, Locke, Montaigne, and Pascal show the shift toward method, sovereignty, subjectivity, and modern inquiry.
- **18th–19th Century and Beyond:** Hume, Kant, Rousseau, Mill, Nietzsche, Austen, and Shelley ensure robust post-1700 presence.

## Literature Balance
- Major literary works appear throughout: Homer, Sophocles, Virgil, Dante, Shakespeare, Austen, Shelley, and biblical wisdom literature.
- Literature is deliberately subordinate: each work dramatizes or tests a philosophical conflict rather than becoming the organizing spine.
- No unit becomes a sprawling literary survey; every literary selection has a specific argumentative purpose.

## Coherent Intellectual Arc
- **Unit 1:** Can we know truth?
- **Unit 2:** If truth exists, can we freely respond to it?
- **Unit 3:** If we are responsible, what kind of soul should we form?
- **Unit 4:** How should formed souls live under law and authority?
- **Unit 5:** What bonds and duties join persons together?
- **Unit 6:** Can love, virtue, and philosophy endure suffering and death?
- **Unit 7:** What final account of human purpose can answer the whole year?

## CUE Alignment
- Each unit is lean, question-driven, and laddered across eras.
- Each unit can later become a CUE package, but this plan stops at the scope-and-sequence level.
- Planned deliverables stay high-level: briefs, reading ladders, handout plans, argument maps, seminar prompts, and final product prompts.

# 5. Decisions Wayne Needs to Make

- **Unit count and pacing:** Approve seven units across 34 weeks, or compress to six larger units for more writing time.
- **Literary load:** Decide whether each unit should use one major literary anchor or two shorter/substantial excerpts.
- **Shakespeare emphasis:** Approve using Shakespeare in three places (_The Tempest_, _Macbeth_, _Julius Caesar_, _King Lear_ with selected scenes), or reduce to two plays.
- **Medieval Christian weight:** Approve the strong Augustine/Aquinas/Boethius/Dante presence, or shift some medieval slots toward Islamic/Jewish medieval voices such as Maimonides if desired.
- **Modern provocations:** Decide how much Nietzsche to include for 10th grade; he is powerful but requires careful excerpting.
- **Capstone expectation:** Approve a full capstone argument paper in Weeks 29–34, or replace it with a seminar defense plus shorter written synthesis.
- **First CUE build:** Choose which unit should be developed first after approval. The strongest candidates are Unit 1 “Do We Love Shadows?”, Unit 2 “Are We Free?”, or Unit 4 “What Makes Rule Legitimate?”
